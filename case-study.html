OVERVIEW

The Problem

We have observed that busy adults are looking for more productive ways to use their limited free time and believe a stronger vocabulary will improve their daily communications. How might we develop an app to be an easy solution for eager individuals to enhance their day to day vocabulary so that they may learn one word a day.

My Role

I was responsible for the entire design process of this project, from defining the problem to delivery. I led communication among my team to ensure we completed our tasks with time to iterate together before moving forward.

RESEARCH

Understanding the User

We have observed that busy adults are looking for more productive ways to use their limited free time and believe a stronger vocabulary will improve their daily communications. How might we develop an app to be an easy solution for eager individuals to enhance their day to day vocabulary so that they may learn one word a day.

Pain Points: Users felt learning apps need to be easy to navigate, so how can we minimize the screens to get the job done? Users also found creating habits difficult so we need to focus on making the reminder notifications personalized and intriguing.

Wants: Users want something that easy to pick up and put down so how can we make it quick, engaging, and have numerous save points?

Personality/Likes: Users have different learning styles and like learning that is fun and does not feel like a chore.

The User Scenario

Melissa, a busy mom of 3, wants to limit her mindless scrolling through social media and use her limited free time between errands to feel like she is improving herself.

She needs....

  • A lean and easy to navigate learning app that is flexible with her schedule
  • Personalized reminders to pop up when she will actually have a moment to interact with the app
  • Engaging and interactive games to help her retain what she is learning

IDEATION

The User Flow

With our user flow, we drew out how a user will go from receiving a notification to learning a new word. We included fail states to better understand where dead-ends can occur and to avoid allowing users to perform a retention quiz if they answered incorrectly too many times. After completion of today’s word there are continued learning options available. Otherwise, users will be reminded to learn again by their next personalized notification.

Notifications: Receive a personalized reminder to learn today’s new word

Fail States: Barred from continuing with a retention quiz and earning a star if they answered incorrectly during the games

Continue Learning: Users can always continue learning new words if they have the time

DESIGN

Variety of Games

After deciding the paths a user can take through our app, we began with low-fidelity designs of each game. Game ideas were whiteboarded and chosen to include several learning types to activitate the brain in differing ways to be as engaging as possible. Start by reading the definition, then tactically spell out the word, next think of what are examples of the word, finally choose between a crossword puzzle or image matching game.

"What is It?"

The crossword puzzle and image matcher are compilation games, to test the user on previously learned words and is unlocked once a user has completed the first three games with 5 words. They provide more hints in case time has passed since they last logged in. Retention is the goal of a learning app so resurfacing old information is crucial for users to feel as if they are learning.

User Tested Improvements

I tested 2 users and my team tested 3 additional to help us find flaws, inconsistencies, and confusing content in our lo-fi design. Our defined objective was, can users successfully learn our word with one play through? We focused on how the user navigates through our screens and where they had difficulty and why. After our tests, we gathered the data on Miro, prioritized the feedback, and redlined the proposed changes.
